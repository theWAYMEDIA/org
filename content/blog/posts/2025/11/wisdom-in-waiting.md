The bank's plastic chairs have been polished smooth by countless bodies shifting in mild discomfort. My lower back sends small twinges of protest as I adjust position — that particular pain that comes from furniture designed to be adequate rather than comfortable. The air conditioning works too well, creating an artificial chill that makes my nose run slightly. I don't bother hiding this small bodily rebellion against manufactured environments, simply using my handkerchief without shame or pretense.

Around me, life unfolds in that particular way that only happens in spaces of shared inconvenience. A mother breastfeeds her infant without ceremony, her body providing what is needed despite the public setting. An elderly man's stomach growls audibly, that honest sound that reminds us bodies don't respect institutional timing. The young security guard shifts from foot to foot, his bladder perhaps sending its own urgent messages that his position doesn't allow him to address immediately.

"Three more hours, probably," says Miguel, who has joined me on this expedition to resolve an issue with my Mexican bank account. His young face holds that particular expression of someone caught between cultures — half apologetic about his country's inefficiency, half defensive against foreign judgment. A gecko watches us from a high corner of the ceiling, perhaps wondering why humans would choose to gather in such sterile conditions when the sun shines so beautifully outside.

"In Japan, this would take fifteen minutes," I tell him, not as criticism but as simple observation. "But I would learn nothing except how efficiently forms can be processed."

The woman beside me shifts slightly, creating more space on the bench we share — that unconscious kindness that emerges naturally when humans occupy common space for extended periods. Her phone battery died an hour ago, forcing her into the present moment with the rest of us. Her fingers occasionally reach for the dark screen from habit, then retreat, finding instead the fabric of her skirt, the texture of her hair, the actual physical world around her.

Miguel checks his own phone, that tiny window to elsewhere that allows modern humans to be physically present while mentally absent. "My father refuses to use online banking," he says, scrolling through notifications. "I keep telling him I could set it up on his phone, save him all this waiting."

"And what did he say?" I ask, though something in me already knows the answer. The elderly couple across from us leans forward slightly, not pretending they aren't listening. In shared spaces like this, other people's conversations become public property, a form of collective entertainment and wisdom-gathering.

Miguel's laugh carries that particular blend of exasperation and admiration children often feel toward parents who refuse modern conveniences. "He said if he doesn't come to the bank and see people, doesn't go to the market to buy food in person, he wouldn't know that Maria's son is engaged to a girl from Guadalajara."

The woman beside me nods, understanding immediately. "The wedding will be next spring," she adds, though no one asked her. "My cousin knows the girl's mother. They're already fighting about the guest list."

"And Señor Jose has a new grandson," contributes the elderly man across from us, not needing an invitation to join our conversation. "Nine pounds! A very strong boy." His wife shows us pictures on her phone — not taken from social media but received directly from the proud grandfather during their chance meeting at the pharmacy three days ago.

"And Señora Elizabeth's husband went to Vancouver for his father's funeral," adds a thin man in work clothes who had seemed to be sleeping until this moment. "Very sad. The Canadian government made it difficult for him to get a visa, even for a funeral."

The security guard, still shifting his weight from foot to foot, adds his own piece to this community tapestry: "He returned yesterday. I saw him at the bus station. He said Canadian winters are exactly as cold as everyone claims."

Miguel looks around in amazement, then back at his phone, which suddenly seems to offer a paltry substitute for this rich exchange of community information. The gecko has moved closer, perhaps drawn by the sudden animation in what had been a room of isolated waiting.

"This is exactly what my father said," Miguel admits, slipping his phone into his pocket with a slightly embarrassed gesture. "He asked me how else would he learn these things if he did everything through apps."

My nose runs again in the artificial cold, and I don't pretend it isn't happening. Bodies leak, process, respond to environments — this truth doesn't care about our social discomfort. "Your father understands something about information," I tell Miguel while using my handkerchief. "There's data, which your phone provides in abundance. And then there's wisdom, which can only be gathered through direct contact with other humans."

The elderly woman across from us nods vigorously. "Algorithms don't tell you that Maria's future daughter-in-law has a kind way with her grandmother's dementia," she says. "Or that Señor Jose was worried about being a grandfather because his own father was so harsh."

"Or that Elizabeth's husband wept when describing the Canadian mountains," adds the thin man who had seemed to be sleeping. "Some things are only shared face to face, soul to soul."

My back twinges again as I shift position, my body's reminder that comfort and convenience aren't always the most valuable currencies. The gecko makes a small sound, perhaps its own commentary on human habits. The security guard finally gets his replacement and hurries to the restroom, his body's needs temporarily overriding institutional requirements.

"In my fish market in Kamakura," I tell Miguel, "we have no waiting numbers, no formal system. Just people gathering, buying, talking. Last year, a child went missing in our town. It was the market network that found her — not through social media alerts but through the fishmonger who remembered seeing her with an unfamiliar adult, the seaweed seller who recalled which direction they walked, the rice vendor who noticed them boarding a specific bus."

"Like human surveillance cameras," Miguel suggests.

"No," the woman beside me corrects before I can. "Like community. Like people who pay attention because they know attention is a form of care."

The bank clerk calls another number, and an elderly man shuffles forward. His transaction takes fifteen minutes — not because it's complicated but because he shares news of his daughter's promotion, asks after the clerk's mother who had been ill, receives advice about a rash on his arm from another waiting customer who recognises the symptoms. Information flows in all directions, without algorithms or filters or screens to mediate the exchange.

Miguel watches all this with growing awareness. The woman beside me has stopped reaching for her dead phone entirely, fully present in this moment of shared waiting. The gecko has settled into a spot where afternoon sun creates a small pool of warmth on the institutional wall.

"Efficiency isn't always the highest value," I tell Miguel as another hour of waiting stretches before us. "Sometimes the wisdom is in the waiting itself, in the forced communion with strangers who become, briefly, a kind of family united by common inconvenience."

He looks around the bank with new eyes — seeing not just inconvenience but connection, not just inefficiency but a community information network that no app could replicate. His phone remains in his pocket, suddenly less compelling than the living exchange happening around him.

When my number is finally called, I almost regret the ending of this shared experience. The elderly couple wishes me luck with my transaction. The woman beside me feels comfortable enough to ask if I need any help translating banking terms. The thin man who had seemed to be sleeping offers advice about which forms will be required. We've become, in these hours of waiting, something more than strangers but less than friends — a temporary community created by shared patience.

As I walk out later, my banking issue resolved, I pass Miguel speaking with his father on the phone. "You were right, Papa," I hear him say. "I learned more waiting in line today than from all my apps this week." The gecko watches from its sun-warmed spot, perhaps understanding something about the wisdom of stillness, about how some nourishment comes only through patient presence.

That evening, back in my small apartment, my back still aching slightly from those institutional chairs, I think about the different hungers we carry — for efficiency, yes, but also for connection, for being recognised, for exchanging the small details that weave community. My nose has stopped running now that I'm away from artificial cold, but my body remembers the experience, carries it in slight discomfort that will fade by morning.

Miguel's father knows something that many of us forget in our rush toward convenience — that sometimes the most valuable information isn't found through the fastest channels, that wisdom often comes through inconvenience rather than ease, that community knowledge requires physical presence in all its inefficient, uncomfortable, beautifully human reality.

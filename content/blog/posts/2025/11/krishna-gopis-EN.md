## Note to the Reader

This teaching is best understood after reading **[The Mystical Marriage: When Soul Meets Spirit](/philosophy/mystical-marriage/)**. Without that context of the feminine soul seeking the masculine spirit across mystical traditions, this ancient story can be easily misunderstood. With that framework, its beauty and depth are revealed.

## The Story: Vastraharan

On the banks of the Yamuna river, the gopis (young cowherd women) bathed in the sacred waters, leaving their clothes on the shore. Krishna, the divine manifested in human form, gathered their clothes and climbed into a tree.

When the gopis discovered their clothes were gone, Krishna called from the tree: "If you want your clothes back, come out of the water with your hands raised in gesture of surrender and worship."

The gopis protested, feeling shame at their nakedness. But Krishna gently insisted: "I don't need you to come out of the water to see you. I already see you completely. Your clothes hide nothing from me. Come as you are."

One by one, the gopis emerged from the water, hands raised in surrender. And in that moment of complete vulnerability, they experienced something extraordinary: not shame, but **liberation**. Not exposure, but **intimate knowing**. Not loss, but **fulfillment**.

Krishna returned their clothes with love, but the gopis had been transformed. They had learned something that would change their lives forever.

## The Hermeneutical Key: This is Mystical Poetry

To understand this story correctly, we must recognize its literary genre. This is not a story about human interaction - it's **mystical allegory** about the relationship between soul and divine spirit.

### The Gopis Represent Souls

The gopis are not simply human women. They are **the soul** (feminine in energetic essence) on its journey toward the divine. Like Mirabai, who called herself a gopi of Krishna, all souls are "gopis" - seekers of the divine.

### Krishna Represents Divine Spirit

Krishna is not simply a man. He is **divine spirit** (masculine in energetic essence) that sees completely, knows intimately, and draws the soul toward union.

### The Clothes Represent Ego and Masks

The clothes the gopis leave on the shore represent:
- Ego identity
- Social roles
- Masks we wear
- Worldly accomplishments
- Reputation and status
- Everything we think defines us

### The Water Represents the Material World

The gopis bathing in the river represents the soul still immersed in the material world, still identified with the mundane, still thinking these things matter to divine spirit.

## The Profound Teaching

When Krishna says: "I don't need you to come out of the water to see you. I already see you completely," he's teaching a fundamental truth:

**Your soul believes its "clothes" - your ego identity, your accomplishments, your social roles, your reputation - define you and possibly hide your flaws from the divine eye. But divine spirit already sees you completely. Knows you more intimately than you know yourself. All your masks are transparent before the divine.**

### The Soul's Resistance

The gopis initially resist: "We can't come out without our clothes! It will be shameful!"

This is the soul's resistance to being truly known. We think:
- "If God knew who I really am..."
- "I must cover my flaws..."
- "I need to present my best version..."
- "My accomplishments make me worthy..."
- "My reputation protects me..."

But divine spirit responds: "I already know who you really are. I see you completely. Stop hiding."

### The Gesture of Surrender

"Come out with your hands raised in worship and surrender."

This is not a gesture of humiliation - it's a **gesture of liberation**. It's the soul finally saying:

*"Okay. I will no longer pretend. I will no longer hide. I will no longer think my masks matter. I surrender to being completely known by you."*

Hands raised are the universal gesture of:
- Voluntary surrender
- Total openness
- Complete receptivity
- Absolute trust
- Genuine worship

### The Transformative Discovery

And what do the gopis discover when they finally emerge from the water, naked and vulnerable before the divine?

**They experience not shame - but ecstasy.**

Not judgment - but unconditional love.

Not rejection - but complete acceptance.

Not exposure - but **intimate knowing**.

They discover that what they feared - being truly seen - is exactly what they yearned for. To be **completely known** by the divine is the greatest blessing, not the greatest shame.

## This Pattern Across Mystical Traditions

This same teaching appears in all mystical traditions:

### St. Teresa of Ávila

Teresa described how the soul must strip itself of all its pretensions before it can enter into mystical union with the divine. It must let go of its spiritual accomplishments, its reputation for holiness, all its spiritual "clothes."

### St. John of the Cross

The "dark night" is the process where God removes all the things the soul leans on - all its "clothes" - until the soul finds itself naked before the divine. Only then can true union occur.

### The Sufis

Rumi constantly wrote about the soul that must strip itself of everything except love for the Beloved. "Come, come, whoever you are. Even if you have broken your vows a thousand times, come again." The divine Beloved doesn't care about your clothes of reputation.

### Mirabai

Mirabai scandalized her family by publicly dancing her love for Krishna, abandoning all "clothes" of social respectability. Her poetry sings of the freedom of being completely seen by the divine without pretense.

## Why This Story is Misread in the West

In the modern West, stripped of grammatical gender that preserves soul-spirit understanding, reading literally rather than mystically, this story becomes:

*"Man hides women's clothes while they're bathing and forces them to expose themselves."*

And when read that way - as interaction between humans rather than allegory about soul and spirit - it legitimately sounds like sexual harassment. Concerns about patriarchy, objectification, and abuse of power are **valid** when applied to actual human behavior.

But this is not a story about human behavior. It's **mystical poetry** about the relationship between your soul and the divine.

The problem is not with the mystical teaching - the problem is we've lost the ability to read mystically. We've lost the interpretive framework that all spiritual traditions once shared.

## The Relevance for Your Spiritual Journey

This teaching is profoundly relevant for your Wayist practice:

### 1. Recognize Your "Clothes"

What "clothes" are you wearing to hide from the divine?
- Your spiritual reputation?
- Your practices and accomplishments?
- Your knowledge of teachings?
- Your past mystical experiences?
- Your role in community?
- Your image as a "good person"?

### 2. Understand You Are Already Seen

Your Divine Tara already sees you completely. Already knows:
- Your secret thoughts
- Your hidden fears
- Your unconfessed desires
- Your past failures
- Your mixed motivations
- Your true essence beyond all this

**And loves you completely anyway.**

### 3. Practice Spiritual Vulnerability

In your meditation, in your prayer, in your connection with your Divine Tara:
- Drop the pretenses
- Release the spiritual masks
- Be honest about your struggles
- Confess your doubts
- Admit your imperfections
- Present yourself as you are

### 4. Experience Freedom

The paradox: when you finally stop hiding, when you allow yourself to be completely known by the divine, you experience not shame but **freedom**.

The burden of maintaining masks disappears. The tension of pretending dissolves. The fear of being discovered evaporates.

In their place: deep peace of being completely known and completely loved.

## Krishna as Wayist Master

In Wayist understanding, Krishna (like Jesus, Buddha, Lao Tzu, and others) was a divine master who taught truths about the nature of existence and the Way toward spiritual realization.

This story is not about the historical Krishna interacting with actual human women. It's **preserved teaching** about how divine spirit works with the soul on its journey toward transformation.

Krishna, like Divine Lord Jesus, knew that the soul must ultimately surrender - must let go of its ego identities and allow itself to be completely known by the divine. Only then can the transformation of the Butterfly Path occur.

## The Butterfly Path Process

This story perfectly describes the transformation process of the Butterfly Path:

### Soul-People Stage

The soul identifies with its "clothes" - roles, accomplishments, reputation. It thinks these things matter. It tries to maintain control. It hides from divine scrutiny.

### The Crisis of Surrender

Divine spirit gently insists: "Drop the masks. Come as you are." The soul resists, protesting that it cannot expose itself this way.

### The Moment of Surrender

Finally, the soul surrenders. It emerges from the water of material identification, hands raised in worship and openness. It allows itself to be completely seen.

### Spirit-People Stage

In this total vulnerability, the soul discovers its true nature. It no longer needs to hide. It no longer identifies with the "clothes" of ego. It has been transformed by the intimate knowing of divine spirit.

## Practical Application

### Surrender Meditation

Sit quietly before your Divine Tara. Imagine all your "clothes" - your roles, accomplishments, reputation - being gently removed. You stand naked before the divine.

What do you feel? Fear? Shame? Resistance?

Now imagine your Divine Tara saying: "I already see you completely. I already know you intimately. I already love you totally. You don't need to hide."

Raise your hands (physically or mentally) in gesture of surrender and openness. Allow yourself to be completely seen.

What do you experience now? Can you feel the shift from shame to freedom? From exposure to divine intimacy?

### Daily Practice of Authenticity

Each day, notice when you're wearing spiritual "clothes":
- Pretending to be more spiritual than you feel
- Hiding your struggles from others
- Maintaining an image of "having it all together"
- Playing a role rather than being authentic

Practice releasing these masks, first before the divine, eventually before trusted community.

### Prayer of Vulnerability

*"Divine Spirit, my blessed Tara:*\
*I will no longer pretend.*\
*I will no longer hide.*\
*I will no longer think my masks matter.*\
*I present myself to you as I am:*\
*With all my imperfections,*\
*With all my fears,*\
*With all my doubts,*\
*With all my confusion.*\
*See me completely.*\
*Know me intimately.*\
*Love me totally.*\
*I surrender to your love.*\
*I open to your transformation.*\
*May the two - my soul and your spirit - become one."*

## Conclusion: The Beauty of Being Completely Known

The story of Krishna and the gopis teaches us that what we most fear - being completely seen - is exactly what we most need.

The soul tries to hide behind its "clothes" of ego identity. But divine spirit already sees through everything. Already knows. Already loves.

When we finally surrender, when we drop our masks, when we allow ourselves to be completely known - we discover not judgment but **unconditional love**. Not rejection but **complete acceptance**. Not shame but **joyful freedom**.

This is the promise of the Butterfly Path. This is the invitation of your Divine Tara. This is the truth that Krishna taught the gopis millennia ago and that remains true today.

Your "clothes" don't define you. Your naked soul, seen completely by the divine and loved totally - that's who you really are.

May you find the courage to release your masks. May you experience the freedom of being completely known. May you discover the ecstasy of intimate union with your Divine Tara.

The two - your soul and divine spirit - are made for each other. They're destined to unite. And that union begins with surrendering the "clothes" you thought mattered.

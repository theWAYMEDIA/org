This morning, while arranging my fish on ice, I watched a young anthropologist interview Old Maria about "traditional market practices." Her tablet was ready to record and categorise, to turn living wisdom into academic data points. But she missed how Maria's hands never stopped moving as she spoke — sorting chiles with that particular rhythm that matches heartbeats, that echoes the same motion I learned in Tsukiji market, that I've seen in Istanbul's spice bazaar and Durban's fish stalls.

Some languages need no translation. The way a vendor tilts their head to acknowledge a regular customer carries the same meaning whether in Bangkok or Barcelona. That subtle nod that says: I remember what you like, I see you, I hold your preferences in my body's knowing. The anthropologist kept asking about "customer retention strategies," missing how Maria was already demonstrating something older than commerce — the choreography of human care that markets have always taught.

The torrential summer rain drums on our metal roof, creating that specific resonance that makes conversation impossible. We all adjust without speaking — vendors shifting closer to share words with customers, bodies learning new proximities, recreating patterns I've seen in rainy season markets from Osaka to Lima. The anthropologist frets about recording quality. She doesn't see how the rain itself is part of the market's language, how it teaches us to move together, to accommodate, to find new ways of connecting.

A child drops a coin that rolls under my counter. Three vendors automatically shift position to track its movement, bodies responding to that particular ring of dropped metal that sounds the same in every market I've known. The anthropologist is asking about "informal economic networks," but she's missing the deeper web — how markets teach us to move as one body, how we learn to catch each other's falling things, to guard each other's interests without needing to be asked.

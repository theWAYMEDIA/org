This morning, rushing to get my trash bags to the curb just as the truck rounded the corner, I heard that familiar symphony — the grinding gears, the hydraulic gasps of the compactor, the easy banter of workers who turn necessity into community ritual. Walking back, I turned to watch their practiced choreography, loading bag after bag into that great mechanical mouth.

And suddenly I was back in Siem Reap, watching Pierre …

Pierre. Now there was someone who should never have found himself sitting barefoot on a Khmer woven mat under the shade of a magnificent mango tree. Let alone at a meditation center on a dusty road, just off the main road to Angkor Wat temples. Old French money, the kind that flows through generations — his family had been wealthy since before revolutions were fashionable, and now the historical medieval village of Monpazier is a national treasure, drawing busloads of day-visitors. His restaurant operated from the converted stables of his family's estate.

What came with that heritage was an attitude. Pierre expected the world to arrange itself around his comfort. When it didn't, like when customers dared ask for salt (questioning his culinary skills?), or when staff moved too slowly during rush hour, he would explode with the fury of someone unaccustomed to life's small rebellions.

His girlfriend (let's call her Chloe) had convinced him to come to our Wayist Spiritual Energy Healing retreat. She was one of those all-knowing types who corrected my terminology with the utmost confidence. I could only smile… Here I was, teaching Wayist practices I'd learned under +Yusa in Indian ashrams, and she's telling me I'm doing it wrong according to some spiritual website she'd discovered.

Pierre arrived like a water buffalo with a toothache (one of the other participants' observations, not mine, but perfectly accurate). He glowered from beneath thunderous brows, radiating the kind of contempt that comes from generations of believing your bloodline makes you inherently superior to everyone else in the room.

The retreat center was a modern house by local standards, nothing fancy by Western measures, but solid and functional. The entire yard was swept dirt under massive trees that provided shade all day, nothing growing beneath their generous canopy. Seven participants in total, including the mismatched couple. We'd start each morning with Karman Meditation with 14 active postures practiced barefoot. Followed by 20+ minutes of a heart sutra Avalokane meditation (Avalokane is the advanced practice that requires spirit-minds rather than concentration).

Siem Reap has her own rhythm, her own essence of life lived authentically. The trash collectors embodied this perfectly. Twice a week, around dawn, they'd gather refuse from the entire dusty road, about a kilometer long, at a collection point maybe 100 meters from our gate. The truck had to back all the way down our narrow lane, with nowhere to turn around, while one worker stood on the roof lifting electrical lines with a long pole as they passed underneath.

These men turned their work into celebration. Jovial voices calling warnings about the wires and branches, advising the driver a little to the left, good-natured teasing about who could throw bags the farthest, genuine pride in keeping their city clean. They approached their task with the kind of dignity that money can't buy, and breeding can't teach.

Saturday morning, our final meditation of the retreat. We'd settled into our circle, feet dusty from the Karman practice, cushions arranged on those beautiful hand-woven mats. Meditation mid-way, the mantra guiding, you could almost sense the spiritual energy flowing, when I heard the familiar rumble of the trash truck arriving late.

My heart sank. Pierre had been 'resistant' throughout the week, listening with grudging attention, making minimal comments, but clearly wrestling with being in a place so far beneath his usual standards. This interruption would surely trigger one of his explosive reactions.

I found myself focusing energy on Pierre, almost sensing I was holding his hand across the circle. I just knew this would blow it for him — all the careful progress of the week undone by mechanical noise and workers' voices.

The truck backed slowly down our lane, hydraulics hissing, voices calling cheerfully. Each bag hit the compactor with a satisfying thud, followed by the grinding crush as the machine did its work. The workers kept up their running commentary — careful of the wires, watch that branch, pass me another bag.

Then the engine revved, gears shifted, and the truck pulled away, leaving us in the sudden quiet. Birdsong returned to replace the noise once again complimenting the mantra, tranquility returned after the mechanical trash symphony.

As our meditation ended and we slowly opened our eyes, I glanced around the circle, everyone looked peaceful, centered. When my gaze reached Pierre, I saw tears streaming down his face — not the angry tears of frustration, but something else entirely. He made no effort to hide them, just sat there letting them flow.

"That," he said quietly, "was the most profound experience of my life."

The rest of us sat in stunned silence.

"At first," he continued, "I was furious. How inconsiderate, how stupid to try to meditate in such racket. But then... it was as if someone took my hand and told me to stay, to wait, to listen."

He paused, wiping his face with the back of his hand — a gesture surprisingly humble and endearing.

"With each bag they threw into that truck, I saw myself loading all the garbage from my heart. Every piece of anger, every resentment, every bitter thought I've been harboring for years. The joy in their voices as they worked, the sunshine, the simple dignity of their task — I felt it all. And when they crushed each load…" He made a pressing motion with his hands. "I felt relief. Real relief. As if years of accumulated garbage was being compressed and taken away. The workers warning shouts to be careful became a personal message, and as the truck was pulling away I sent it off with the 'gate, gate, paragate…' of the mantra echoing in my heart." *(heart sutra mantra 'go, go, go further…')*

"And now it's all gone, all that garbage loaded and crushed and driven away," he whispered.

That morning, our final meditation ended not with spiritual platitudes or mystical insights, but with the most practical spiritual development imaginable — discovering that spiritual potential can arrive disguised as municipal waste management, complete with hydraulic sound effects and workers who understand that even garbage collection can be done with dignity and as a service to the community.

Sometimes theWAY uses whatever's available. Sometimes what's available is a trash truck, arriving precisely when someone's ready to let go of what no longer serves them.

And sometimes the most profound teaching comes from watching people who approach their work, any work, with joy.

Funny how liberation can arrive on the back of a garbage truck, driven by men who already knew the secret one can spend a lifetime refusing to learn: that treating every task, every person, every moment with dignity transforms both the work and the worker.

The compactor does its job regardless of whether you fight it or flow with it.

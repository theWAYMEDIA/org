The morning light catches on señorita Juanita's scissors as they move through my hair, each snip precise despite the slight trembling in her delicate fingers. I watch her in the mirror, studying the new markings on her face — after a trip home — cursive words etched across her cheeks and forehead in uneven strokes. "Carpe diem." "Hold your hope." "Honour is everything." Not embellishments but incantations, prayers made visible against skin that has already served as canvas for older stories.

The salon smells of chemical dyes and cheap coffee, with undercurrents of something sharper — the particular scent of fear struggling against courage. A small gecko watches from behind the mirror, its translucent skin revealing the tiny pulse of its heart. Like Juanita, it knows something about making itself both visible and invisible according to necessity.

When I first met her months ago, I recognised her extensive tattoos not as the casual decorations Westerners often adopt to feel more "authentic" or "tribal," but as something far more essential. The leopard spots climbing her arms, the intricate patterns adorning her neck, back and chest — these were not fashion statements but fortifications, barriers constructed with ink and pain against memories and disappointments too heavy to carry unprotected.

Today, something has changed. The new markings on her face bear none of the careful artistry of her earlier work. They appear almost desperate — hasty incisions into the most visible part of herself, written in the awkward script of someone trying to convince themselves of something they fear might not be true.

"You're going somewhere?" I ask, though I already know the answer from the way she is disengaged with her craft today, how her eyes keep finding the door, as if measuring the distance between where she stands and where she needs to be.

"Back home," she says, the words carrying that particular weight that comes when "home" is both origin and wound. Her scissors pause momentarily, suspended in air like her breath, before resuming their dance through my hair.

We exist in the peculiar intimacy that forms between people whose bodies share space but whose languages remain partially foreign to each other. My Spanish limps along with too many Japanese cadences; her English carries the particular hesitation of someone who learned it through necessity rather than choice. Yet in the silence between our limited vocabularies, I read the story written in her new tattoos, in the slight tremor of her hands, in the way she keeps checking herself to make sure she's still here.

Later, her text message confirms what her body has already told me. She must return to whatever circumstances she once fled, to whatever reality necessitated the first layers of inked protection. The new facial tattoos make sudden, terrible sense — not decoration but declaration, not beauty but boundary. Visible reminders to herself of what she must hold onto when returning to places — physical and emotional — that once threatened to unmake her.

I think about how we humans create our own medicines against life's toxins. How some build walls of credentials or wealth, how others weave protective garments of ideology or dogma. Juanita's medicine is more honest — visible marks that simultaneously reveal and conceal, that proclaim "I have been wounded" while also declaring "I am still here."

The gecko has moved now, skittering to a new vantage point where light falls differently. Its ability to change colour slightly to match its surroundings seems both similar to and different from Juanita's transformations. Both are adaptations for survival, but the gecko's changes are instinctual while Juanita's carry the weight of conscious choice, of deliberate pain embraced as alternative to deeper suffering.

What can I offer someone whose battles I can barely comprehend, whose courage speaks through ink when words fail? Not advice, certainly. Not the hollow reassurances that come so easily to those who haven't had to fortify their skin against their past. Perhaps only recognition — the simple acknowledgment that I see both her wounds and her strength, that I witness her efforts to create herself anew even as circumstances pull her backward.

Tonight, as I run my fingers through newly cut hair, I think about how Juanita's tattoos serve as both armour and map — protecting her from what has been while guiding her toward what might still be. The gecko that watched our exchange from the salon wall understood something I'm still learning — that survival sometimes requires making yourself more visible precisely when instinct tells you to hide, that sometimes the most protective colouring is not camouflage but declaration.

We have an energy connection, so I will use it. I send her goodwill through the invisible connections that bind all living things, hoping it reaches her like light finding its way through cracks. Whatever demons wait for her, may she remember that the medicine she's inscribed upon herself isn't just decoration but invocation — calling forth the strength she'll need, marking boundaries that others must respect, creating herself again and again through deliberate transformation. I vowed to myself to remain her energy friend.

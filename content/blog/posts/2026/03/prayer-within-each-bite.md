The morning rain left Puerto Vallarta's cobblestones slick with that particular wetness that holds light differently than dry stone. My sandals make soft slapping sounds as I navigate to my favourite mariscos stand, where Carlos prepares fish with hands that know exactly how much lime each species requires. My stomach growls audibly — that honest sound bodies make when they recognise nourishment approaches. I don't silence it or pretend it didn't happen. The body speaks its own truths, regardless of our social discomfort.

Carlos notices me coming and already reaches for the red snapper he set aside — our wordless agreement formed over weeks of my winter residence. On the counter sits a small jar of his homemade salsa macha, the chile oil floating on top like sunrise caught in glass. A gecko watches from beneath the plastic tarp roof, its throat pulsing with what might be anticipation or simple existence — the line between the two often thinner than we acknowledge.

"¿Para llevar?" Carlos asks, though he already knows I'll eat here, at the small blue table that catches both shade and sea breeze.

"No, aquí," I confirm, settling onto the plastic chair that has moulded itself to countless bodies before mine. My knees crackle slightly as I sit — another bodily truth announcing itself without concern for my dignity. The table wobbles on uneven legs, a physical metaphor for how we all find our balance on imperfect foundations.

Carlos brings the fish, perfectly grilled, skin crackling with tiny bubbles of fat that catch light like miniature lanterns. Beside it sits rice with flecks of cilantro, beans the deep purple-black of midnight oceans, and tortillas wrapped in cloth to hold their warmth. The gecko has moved closer, perhaps drawn by scent or simply following its own mysterious agenda.

I arrange my space, positioning each element precisely — not from fussiness but from the particular attention that transforms eating from mere consumption into relationship. My hands smooth the napkin across my lap, inspect the fork's tines, test the tortilla's warmth against my palm. The woman at the next table — American by her accent, perhaps mid-forties with skin that speaks of recent arrival to tropical sun — watches me with growing curiosity.

I lift my first bite, inspecting it momentarily. The fish's flesh parts in perfect flakes, its moisture catching light. I notice the precise point where heat transformed its cellular structure, how the grill marks create both flavour and visual rhythm. I inhale deliberately, letting the aroma enter my senses before the food itself. My eyes close briefly as I place it in my mouth — not in prayer as she might recognise it, but in full presence.

Each chew is intentional, my tongue finding salt crystals, lime's bright corners, the particular umami that speaks of a fish that died well and recently. My jaw works slowly, thoroughly, finding flavours that hasty eating would miss entirely. I follow the food's journey beyond my mouth, sensing its progress through my body — the warming of my stomach, the slight relaxation in my shoulders as nourishment begins its work.

When I open my eyes, the American woman is still watching me, her own plate of food sitting largely untouched. Her expression has shifted from curiosity to something like recognition.

"You didn't pray before eating," she says, her voice carrying that particular hesitancy of someone stepping carefully into a stranger's space. "But you're... praying while you eat, aren't you?"

I consider her question, taking another bite and giving it the same complete attention before answering. "Perhaps all prayer is simply complete attention," I suggest. "The divine speaks through flavour as clearly as through words."

Her face opens like a morning glory sensing dawn. "You're Wayist," she says, not a question but a recognition. "I've been studying the traditions for about three years now. I recognised the... the complete presence you bring to ordinary actions."

My napkin catches a drop of sauce from the corner of my mouth. I don't pretend the messiness of eating doesn't exist, don't hide the body's honest engagement with food. "The Way teaches us that separation between sacred and ordinary is illusion," I say. "How can I thank the sea for this fish if I don't fully experience what it offers?"

She moves to my table without asking, bringing her plate. The gecko scurries away at this new movement, then returns to a different watching spot. Her food — the same fish but with different accompaniments — has been pushed around rather than eaten, as if appetite were secondary to some other purpose.

"I was raised saying grace before meals," she explains, her fingers nervously adjusting her fork's position. "A quick thanks and then we'd eat while talking about everything except the food. It's been hard to break the habit of... of not really tasting anything."

A bead of sweat makes its way down her temple — the honest response of northern skin to tropical heat. She doesn't wipe it away immediately, and I recognise in this small surrender a willingness to let her body exist without constant correction.

"What helped me," I tell her, breaking a piece of tortilla with deliberate attention, "was understanding that the fish didn't die so I could ignore it. The corn didn't grow so I could be distracted during its consumption. Attention is the deepest form of respect."

She tries it then — picks up a piece of fish, examines it briefly, closes her eyes as she places it in her mouth. Her jaw moves slowly, deliberately. When her eyes open, they hold surprise and something deeper — the particular wonder that comes when the ordinary suddenly reveals itself as extraordinary.

"I taste... the ocean," she whispers. "And something almost citrusy in the fish itself, not just the lime they squeezed on it."

"The red snapper feeds on small fish that eat algae," I explain. "What you taste is its entire life, its relationship with its environment. When you pay attention, you consume not just flesh but story."

The gecko has moved closer again, perhaps sensing the shift in energy at our table. The woman notices it and simply nods in acknowledgment, neither shooing it away nor making a fuss about its presence. This small acceptance tells me more about her readiness than any words could.

"I've been trying to understand the Wayist approach to gratitude," she says, taking another bite with new deliberation. "The books explain it intellectually, but seeing you just now — how completely you inhabit the experience of eating — I finally understand it's not about the words at all."

My fingers find a small bone, carefully removing it and placing it at the edge of my plate. "Words create distance," I tell her. "Between you and what you're thanking, between the experience and your understanding of it. What does the fish care for your words? But your complete attention — that is a thank you it can feel, even beyond death."

We eat in shared silence then, the kind that forms its own kind of intimacy. I notice how her posture gradually changes, her spine softening away from the rigid performance of self so many carry through life. Her chewing slows. Her eyes close occasionally to focus on particular flavours. Without trying to mirror me, she naturally finds her own rhythm of attention.

Carlos brings us both water without being asked, the glasses sweating in the humid air. Water drops trace paths down the sides, creating small puddles on the plastic tablecloth — another ordinary miracle usually overlooked. I drink deliberately, feeling coolness spread through my chest, tracking the water's journey as it quenches cells along its path.

"I've spent three years studying Wayism," she says eventually, "and learned more watching you eat fish for ten minutes than from all my reading."

The gecko makes a small sound, almost like agreement. My napkin finds another spot of sauce, this time on my finger. The honesty of eating — its messiness, its sensuality, its complete engagement with physical reality — contains more spiritual teaching than many temples I've visited.

"The Path is not found in books," I remind her, "but in complete attention to whatever is before you. The divine speaks through fish bones and sweat drops as clearly as through scripture, if we learn to listen with our whole being."

"You're from Japan, aren't you?" she asks. "I recognise the accent. I visited Kamakura once — the temples were beautiful."

"I sell fish in the Kamakura market," I tell her. "And find as much divine wisdom there as in any temple. Perhaps more, since fish don't pretend to be anything other than what they are."

Her laugh carries that particular delight of finding unexpected connection far from home. "I'm a high school counsellor from Minnesota. My name is Beth."

"Kaori," I offer in return. "Fish vendor and reluctant wisdom-seeker."

"Reluctant?" Her eyebrow raises.

My spoon finds the last grain of rice on my plate, brings it to my mouth with the same attention I gave the first bite. "Truth doesn't ask our permission before revealing itself," I explain. "Sometimes it speaks through fish guts and sweaty foreheads rather than pleasant circumstances. Being willing to receive wisdom wherever it appears can be... inconvenient."

Beth's eyes hold that particular shine that comes when mind and heart align in sudden understanding. "That's why it's called the Way and not the Destination," she says. "It's about how we walk, not where we're trying to get to."

The gecko nods — or perhaps it's just catching its balance. Sometimes the difference doesn't matter.

We finish our meals in companionable silence, each bite given its proper attention, each flavour accorded its moment of recognition. No formal prayer passed our lips, yet I've rarely experienced more sacred communion. The fish has been thoroughly honoured by our complete reception of its offering. The corn, the beans, the hands that prepared them — all thoroughly thanked through our deliberate awareness.

Later, walking back to my apartment as afternoon rain begins again, I think about how wisdom finds us in unexpected places — across wobbling blue tables, through the medium of grilled fish, in the witnessed eating habits of strangers. My sandals make that same slapping sound against wet cobblestones, but now they seem to say *presence, presence, presence* with each step.

The rain soaks through my shirt, plastering cotton to skin. I don't hurry to escape it. Water falling from sky to body is also worthy of complete attention, also a form of prayer when experienced fully. My nose runs slightly from the sudden temperature change — another bodily truth asserting itself without concern for dignity.

Tomorrow, perhaps Beth will join me again at Carlos's stand. Or perhaps our paths will never cross again. Either way, something essential was exchanged over fish and rice — a recognition that transcends friendship or acquaintance. In that moment of shared attention, we both remembered what our bodies never forgot: that gratitude isn't something you say but something you embody, that prayer exists in the space between complete attention and complete surrender, that divine wisdom flows most clearly through unwashed fingers and honest appetites.

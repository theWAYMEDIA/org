The social media influencer arrived at my market stall this morning wearing designer sandals and that particular expression of practised authenticity that comes from too many hours studying one's own face on screens. Her camera was already rolling as she walked, narrating her "discovery" of our "quaint local fish market" to her followers. Her personal lighting technician — because apparently, authenticity requires perfect illumination — trailed behind her like an anxious moth.

I recognised the sound before I saw it happen — that specific wet suction-pop that only comes from expensive rubber meeting fish-slick concrete. The noise carries its own kind of poetry: the initial grip of sole against thin water, the microscopic resistance as weight shifts forward, the sudden release that sends a perfect arc of market water — equal parts fish blood, scale slime, and melted ice — up the back of expensive bare legs.

*pop-splat, pop-splat*

She kept walking, kept talking to her camera about "immersing herself in local culture," seemingly unaware of the baptism happening with each step. The market workers maintained that particular stillness that comes from suppressing joy — only their eyes moving, following each perfectly timed splash. Even the cats paused their morning grooming to watch nature deliver its lesson in physics and humility.

*pop-splat, pop-splat*

Her curated white outfit began collecting evidence of reality — tiny dots of fish water marking each step like a timeline of poor footwear choices. Her lighting technician, wearing sensible boots, tried to warn her. But she was too focused on capturing her "authentic market experience" to notice the increasingly elaborate patterns decorating her back.

*pop-splat, pop-splat*

The fish on my counter watched with their characteristic unblinking attention as she approached my stall. Their eyes held that ancient wisdom about hubris and its consequences, about how sometimes the universe delivers its messages through simple hydraulics. My hands kept moving through their morning work, scales catching light like scattered karma.

"Can you be more... you know, authentic?" she asked me, her camera still rolling. Behind her, a perfect arc of fish water rose in slow motion, catching her lighting just so. Some truths need no translation, no filter, no careful editing to make their point.

*pop-splat*

Tonight, after cleaning my stall, I'll remember her face when she finally noticed — that exact moment when curated reality met market physics. Her followers will never see that footage, I suppose. But the market remembers. The cats remember. The fish water, now dried on expensive white fabric, remembers.

Tomorrow, perhaps another influencer will arrive, seeking authenticity in all the wrong places. The market floor will be waiting, carrying its thin film of ancient wisdom, ready to teach its lessons one wet slap at a time.

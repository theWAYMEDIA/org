The politician's shoes click importantly across our market's worn concrete — Italian leather that's never known fish scales or salt water. His campaign visit was announced yesterday, so the aisles have been swept extra clean, though you can't really sanitise the honest smell of the sea. His smile has that particular brightness that comes from practising in mirrors, his handshake that calculated pressure that pretends intimacy while maintaining distance.

Old Chen watches from his stall, his hands moving steadily through the work of scaling fish. In fifteen years, I've never heard him speak. His throat bears burn scars from some untold story, but his silence feels chosen rather than forced. His fingers know exactly how to angle each fish, how to let scales fall in perfect patterns that tell their own kind of truth.

"We need to modernise this market," the politician announces to the cameras that trail him like faithful dogs. "Bring it into the twenty-first century!" His voice carries that peculiar emptiness of words chosen for sound rather than meaning. Behind him, Chen's knife slides through flesh with the precision that comes from decades of relationship with fish and sea and sustenance. No wasted motion, no unnecessary flourish.

The politician's aide googled "fish market customs" this morning — I can tell by how he carefully mirrors the gestures of other customers, his movements a fraction of a second delayed, like a badly synced translation. He helps his boss examine my display with studied interest. "The people want progress," the politician declares, gesturing at my traditional wooden cutting board, my well-worn knives. "They want innovation, modernisation!"

Chen's eyes meet mine briefly over the politician's shoulder. In that glance lies decades of watching tides turn, seasons change, fish grow scarce, then abundant again. His hands never stop their steady work — each movement a meditation, each slice a sermon more profound than any campaign speech.

The politician buys nothing but has his picture taken holding a large tuna, his suit carefully arranged to suggest both authority and approachability. The fish's eye stares blankly, perhaps recognising something of itself in this man's emptiness. Chen continues his work, his silence heavy with the kind of wisdom that comes through watching rather than speaking, through doing rather than promising.

Later, after the cameras have gone, I watch Chen clean his stall with the same careful attention he gives to each fish. His movements carry that particular grace that comes from souls who have walked many roads, lived many lives. The politician's voice still echoes off market walls — promises of progress, of change, of better things to come. But Chen's silence holds something older than promises, deeper than words.

I remember what my fish market teacher used to say about souls and their journey — how some must learn through noise and motion, through the clash and clamour of ambition, while others have already found the quiet centre where true power lives. The politician's quick smile and quicker words mark him as a soul still learning through action and reaction, through the loud lessons of public life. But Chen's scarred hands and chosen silence speak of a spirit that has learned enough to value the spaces between words, the wisdom in simply being.

Tonight, as I clean my own stall, I think about how we measure progress, how we judge wisdom. The politician's words will appear in tomorrow's papers, carefully edited to remove their emptiness. Chen's silence will continue its profound teaching for those who have learned to listen with more than ears. His scales will still shimmer on the concrete, catching light like scattered truth, while campaign promises fade like morning mist.

The market cats know something about this. They've always preferred Chen's quiet corner, gathering around his feet as he works, recognising the kind of strength that needs no voice to make itself known. They avoid the loud and flashy, having learned through countless generations that true worth often speaks most clearly through silence.

Before heading home, Chen leaves a perfect fish on my counter — his daily gesture of connection that needs no words to carry its meaning. The politician's business card sits in my pocket, thick with empty promises printed on expensive paper. Two kinds of communication, two stages of soul development, two ways of moving through the world — one noise, one signal; one surface, one depth.

Tomorrow, the papers will quote the politician's vision for our market's future. Chen will continue his quiet work, his hands speaking volumes about present reality, about the kind of progress that comes through perfecting simple tasks rather than grand declarations. His silence will teach anyone willing to learn about the weight of words, about the wisdom of watching, about how some souls grow loud while others grow deep.

The market's evening light catches on fish scales scattered during the day's work — some from Chen's careful hands, some from the politician's photo opportunity. They shine with equal brightness, but tell very different stories about the hands that released them, about the souls that chose either to speak or to stay silent, about the many paths wisdom takes on its long journey home.

# The Light You're Not Getting

*Why the most ancient spiritual teaching about divine radiance turns out to be the most urgent health advice you'll hear this year*

---

Right now, as you read these words, your brain is spending energy it stored from sunlight. Not metaphorically. Not poetically. Photons that entered your eyes over the past few days triggered a cascade of chemistry that produced the serotonin your brain is currently burning through to make sense of this sentence. If you've been indoors for most of the past week — and statistically, you probably have — your brain is running on fumes. Your thinking is slower than it should be. Your mood is lower than it needs to be. And tonight, you'll probably sleep badly, again, for reasons you'll blame on stress or age or the news.

It's not stress. It's not age. It's photons. You're not getting enough of them.

For thousands of years, Wayist practitioners have chanted the Gayatri Dharani — one of the oldest prayers in human history — asking divine light to illuminate their minds:

> *Tat savitur vareṇyaṃ*
> *Bhargo devasya dhīmahi*
> *Dhiyo yo naḥ pracodayāt*
>
> *Divine light most excellent*
> *On this radiance we meditate*
> *May it illuminate our minds*

The ancient teachers who composed this prayer understood something that modern science is only now catching up to: light doesn't just illuminate the world around us. It builds us. It runs us. Without it, we break down — body, soul-minds, and spirit.

This isn't a metaphor. This is photochemistry. And it's personal.

## Your Skin Is a Pharmaceutical Factory

There's a molecule sitting in your skin right now called 7-dehydrocholesterol. It's been there your whole life. It has one job: wait for ultraviolet B photons from the sun, and when they arrive, convert itself into what we call vitamin D — though "vitamin" is a misnomer. It's actually a hormone, and it regulates over a thousand genes in your body. Your immune system runs on it. Your bones depend on it. Your muscles need it. Your brain requires it.

Here's the problem: if you live above about 35 degrees latitude — roughly the line from Los Angeles to Tokyo — UVB radiation drops dramatically from October through March. The molecule sits in your epidermis through the entire winter, waiting for photons that don't arrive. Your vitamin D levels decline steadily. By late winter, your stores are at their annual minimum. Your immune surveillance is at its weakest. Your bone density loss is at its fastest. And the respiratory infection season peaks at exactly the moment your photon supply runs out.

This isn't coincidence. It's photochemistry constrained by orbital mechanics. The Earth tilts, the photons thin out, and your pharmaceutical factory goes quiet.

Dr. Michael Holick spent years at Boston University documenting this and advocating for sensible sun exposure. The dermatological establishment's position was absolute sun avoidance. Holick's position, supported by the photochemistry and population data, was that the dose matters — that moderate exposure produces essential hormones, and that the people most at risk for deficiency were the people most likely to stay indoors. He was removed from the dermatology department for saying what the physics showed.

The vitamin D deficiency epidemic among adults over 65 — estimated at 40 to 60 percent in northern latitudes — is the evidence his career predicted.

### Your Skin Colour Is Calibrated to Where Your Ancestors Lived

Pigmentation adds a dimension to this that the physics makes unavoidable, and it's worth understanding clearly because it affects real health outcomes.

Melanin — the pigment that determines skin colour — absorbs UVB photons before they reach the 7-dehydrocholesterol deeper in the epidermis. Darker skin contains more melanin and therefore requires longer sun exposure to produce the same amount of vitamin D. A person with very dark skin may need three to five times more exposure than a person with very light skin to produce the same quantity.

This isn't a flaw. It's an evolutionary calibration. Populations that developed near the equator, where UVB is intense year-round, developed high melanin as protection against DNA damage. Populations that migrated to higher latitudes, where UVB is scarce, developed lighter skin to allow scarce photons through to the vitamin D factory. The physics matched the geography.

When people with high-melanin skin live at northern latitudes — as millions do — the mismatch creates a compounded deficit: less UVB available from the sky, and more melanin filtering what does arrive. Vitamin D deficiency rates in dark-skinned populations at northern latitudes are significantly higher than in light-skinned populations at the same latitude. The photochemistry doesn't negotiate with social circumstances. It follows the physics of absorption.

The practical takeaway is simple: know your skin, know your latitude, and adjust your sun exposure accordingly. If you have dark skin and live far from the equator, you need more deliberate time outdoors — not less.

## The Clock in Your Eyes

Now here's where it gets personal in a way you'll feel tonight.

Sunlight is doing something to your eyes that has nothing to do with seeing. Buried in your retina, behind the rods and cones that give you vision, are specialised cells that most people have never heard of: **intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells**. They were discovered by David Berson at Brown University in 2002. These cells contain a pigment called melanopsin, tuned specifically to blue light at a wavelength of approximately 480 nanometres.

They don't contribute to your visual experience. You don't see through them. They have a completely different job.

These cells send signals along a dedicated neural pathway — the retinohypothalamic tract — directly to a tiny cluster of roughly 20,000 neurons in your hypothalamus called the **suprachiasmatic nucleus**. This cluster is your master circadian clock. It runs your entire 24-hour biological schedule: when cortisol rises in the morning to wake you, when body temperature peaks in the late afternoon, when digestion activates with daytime eating, when melatonin rises at night to bring sleep. Every physiological rhythm in your body takes its timing from this cluster. And this cluster takes its timing from light entering your eyes through melanopsin.

Here are the numbers that make this personal.

Typical indoor lighting — your living room, your kitchen, your office — produces roughly 100 to 500 lux. The melanopsin cells need approximately 1,000 to 2,000 lux to fire at the intensity required for strong circadian setting. Outdoor shade — not direct sunlight, just standing under a tree on a cloudy day — produces 10,000 to 25,000 lux. Direct sunlight produces 50,000 to 100,000 lux.

The difference between indoors and outdoors is not a percentage. It's an order of magnitude. If you spend your day inside, your circadian clock is receiving one-tenth to one-fiftieth of the signal it needs.

Without the daily morning light signal, your circadian clock drifts. It runs at approximately 24.2 hours instead of 24 — a measured property of the human clock established through isolation experiments where subjects lived without time cues. Two-tenths of an hour sounds trivial. Over a week, it shifts your entire sleep-wake cycle by nearly an hour and a half. Within a month, you'd be sleeping and waking at completely different times than your environment demands. Morning light resets the clock to exactly 24 hours every day, synchronising your internal rhythm to the actual rotation of the Earth.

**If you wake at 3am and can't return to sleep. If you feel drowsy by 7pm. If you never feel fully alert during the day.** The most likely problem is that your circadian clock isn't receiving a strong enough morning light signal.

No supplement does this. No sleeping pill resets the circadian clock. No drug enters your retina and resets the suprachiasmatic nucleus. Photons entering your eyes through melanopsin do. That is the only input the clock accepts.

### It Gets Harder as You Age

As you age, the lens of your eye yellows — a gradual accumulation of chromophores that absorb short-wavelength light. By 65, your lens transmits significantly less blue light than it did at 20. The specific wavelength melanopsin needs — 480 nanometres, deep blue — is precisely the range your ageing lens filters most aggressively.

The clock signal is weakened at both ends. Less time outdoors means fewer photons arriving. A yellowing lens means fewer of the arriving photons reach the melanopsin cells. The result: a circadian system receiving a fraction of the signal it received in your thirties.

Sonia Ancoli-Israel at the University of California San Diego documented this in clinical studies. Increased bright light exposure in older adults improved sleep efficiency, reduced nighttime awakenings, and consolidated the sleep-wake rhythm. The intervention was not a drug. It was light.

Your grandmother knew this. "Go to bed at the same time and get up with the sun" — she was describing circadian entrainment. She just called it common sense.

## Sunlight Makes Serotonin — Directly

Those same melanopsin cells connect to another structure in the brainstem: the raphe nuclei, where serotonin is manufactured. Gregory Lambert and colleagues, publishing in *The Lancet* in 2002, did something remarkable to measure this relationship. They sampled the jugular vein — the vein that drains the brain — and directly measured serotonin turnover in living human subjects in relation to the amount of bright light they'd been exposed to that day.

More bright light meant more serotonin synthesis. The relationship was direct, continuous, and independent of season, temperature, or mood. More photons entering the retina meant more serotonin produced in the brain. The pathway runs through the same melanopsin cells, the same retinohypothalamic tract, branching to the raphe nuclei instead of — or in addition to — the suprachiasmatic nucleus.

So when you spend a day indoors and feel flat, low, unmotivated by evening — that's not a character flaw or a bad attitude. That's a photon deficit. Your serotonin factory didn't get its raw material. Your brain is doing the best it can with what you gave it, which wasn't much.

And when you remember a holiday where you spent days outdoors and felt extraordinarily clear, happy, and alive — that wasn't just "relaxation." Your brain was running on a full tank for the first time in months.

**In the Material Domain**, sunlight drives the photochemistry that produces vitamin D, resets your circadian clock, and manufactures serotonin. Without it, your body deteriorates, your sleep collapses, and your brain dims.

**In the Soul Domain**, the effects of light deficiency show up as mood disruption, emotional flatness, difficulty concentrating, and the vague sense that something is wrong but you can't identify what. These aren't "just" physical symptoms — they're the soul-minds responding to a body that isn't functioning properly. When your serotonin is depleted and your sleep is fragmented, your soul-minds can't do their work of processing life experience into wisdom.

**In the Spirit Domain**, the connection is the most ancient teaching of all. The Gayatri Dharani asks the divine radiance of Savitur — the solar deity — to illuminate our minds, to inspire and guide our spiritual development. This isn't about worshipping the physical sun. It's a recognition that the same principle of radiance operates at every level of existence: the photon that resets your circadian clock and the divine light that illuminates your spirit-minds are expressions of the same fundamental reality.

The Gayatri Dharani brings all three levels together in a single meditation:

This is the Wayist understanding: that your spiritual development — your progress along the Butterfly Path from soul-being toward spirit-being — doesn't happen in some separate mystical compartment disconnected from your biology. It happens *through* your biology. Your body is the Material Domain vehicle your soul uses for this incarnation's curriculum. If that vehicle isn't maintained — if it's starved of the light it was calibrated to receive — then the soul-minds and spirit-minds that depend on it are operating with a handicap.

## Udbhu-Kanti and the Discipline of Going Outside

In Wayist teaching, udbhu-kanti is the philosophy of natural, authentic living — finding beauty and energy in what is real, what is simple, what connects us to the rhythms of the natural world rather than the synthetic substitutes we've been sold.

Going outside isn't glamorous advice. It doesn't sell supplements. It doesn't require an app or a subscription. It's as udbhu-kanti as advice gets: the molecule is sitting in your skin, waiting. The melanopsin cells in your retina are calibrated for a signal that only the sun provides. The serotonin factory in your brainstem needs raw material that only outdoor light delivers. No pill, no screen filter, no supplement replaces the photon.

The Wayist teaching on udbhu-kanti reminds us that the most profound solutions are usually the simplest — and the ones most easily dismissed precisely because they're simple. Walking barefoot on earth. Eating real food. And spending time in natural light — not because it's a nice idea, but because your body is a biological machine that was built, through millions of years of development, to run on sunlight.

When we chant in the Gayatri Dharani, "May the light inspire, may the light develop us" — we're not asking for something exotic. We're asking for something we've been deprived of by the very indoor civilisation we built to protect ourselves.

## What to Do — The Prescription Is Ancient and Simple

**Morning light, every day.** Within the first hour after waking, spend 10 to 20 minutes outside. Not through a window — glass filters the UV spectrum and reduces the light intensity your melanopsin cells need. Outside. Face the general direction of the sky (never stare directly at the sun). On a cloudy day, you're still getting 10,000+ lux. This resets your circadian clock, triggers cortisol at the right time, and begins your serotonin production for the day.

**Midday sun for vitamin D.** Depending on your latitude, skin colour, and season, 10 to 30 minutes of midday sun exposure on arms and legs (without sunscreen, which blocks UVB) is sufficient for vitamin D synthesis. If you have darker skin or live at higher latitudes, you need the longer end of that range. If you're over 65, you need more deliberate exposure because your skin's conversion efficiency decreases with age.

**Honour the sunset.** Your body is designed to register the sun's descent. As dusk settles, your physiology shifts. When artificial light floods your eyes after sunset, your body can't properly transition into its rest phase. Dim the screens. Let the evening be evening.

**Know that this is not optional.** This isn't wellness advice. This isn't a "nice to have." Your circadian rhythm, your immune function, your serotonin production, your bone density, your sleep architecture, and your capacity for spiritual development all depend on the same thing: photons entering your body through your skin and your eyes, from the sun, outdoors.

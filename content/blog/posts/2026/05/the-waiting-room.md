Puerto Vallarta is not any different to Kamakura in how hospital waiting rooms have a particular acoustic cruelty. The architect who designed this place — with its ceramic floors and its metal-plate ceiling — never had to sit here waiting for someone to tell him whether the person he loved most in the world was still alive. If he had, he would have understood that sound rarely consoles. It amplifies.

Every plastic chair squeaks. Every pair of shoes announces its tragedy. The rusted wheel of a gurney coming through the emergency doors arrives as a question — *who? how much?* — and everyone's body in the room leans imperceptibly toward the sound before withdrawing, ashamed of its own fear.

I've been here three hours. A friend is waiting while his wife has surgery. I stayed for company, though he's gone looking for coffee and I'm left with his chair and mine and the collective weight of this place.

My thighs are numb. The plastic backrest has carved its shape into my spine. I'm hungry, and that thought — *hungry, what a shame to be hungry here* — teaches me something about the body's outrageous insistence. Outside, through the window glass, I can see tourists passing in their shorts and cut-off shirts, licking ice creams. The world continues. That too is a kind of cruelty, though innocent. The zanates in the garden trees watch them without opinion. They have seen all of this before.

---

The zanates have been here longer than the hospital. Longer than the street, probably. These trees are theirs in a way no building belongs to anyone — they have claimed them with decades of evening assemblies, with generations of fledglings who learned in these same branches the idiom of human crisis. They have watched the garden fill and empty the way the room inside fills and empties: people arriving carrying the future and leaving carrying something else. They have seen autumn bring sweaters and anxiety. They have seen summer bring tourists with ice creams and anxiety. They have seen midnight weeping and three-in-the-afternoon weeping and the silent weeping of those who have no more tears left.

What they see this evening is not different, in their terms. There is a man standing in the garden. There are tears. This too they have seen before.

But one of them — the one with the most yellow eye, the one with the longest tail — tilts his head. Because there is something in this man that the zanates have not seen many times. Or perhaps they have. But it is uncommon.

---

The room fills and empties like tide. A family arrives: mother, two adult children, a grandmother praying the rosary with fingers who know that road perfectly. A young man arrives with reddened eyes who doesn't even sit — just walks from wall to wall as if movement could hold him together. A middle-aged woman arrives speaking quietly into her phone, telling someone over and over: *I don't know yet, I don't know yet.*

When a doctor emerges, the room transforms. A change in pressure. Bodies straighten, eyes lift, hands release what they were holding — phones, rosaries, coffee sleeves — as if one must be light to receive news, whatever it is. The conversation with the doctor is always the same in its form: the body leaning forward, the intensity of the face, the decisive instant. Then the person returns to their chair carrying something different in their back — either the relief that relaxes, or the weight that bends.

I observe these small and enormous transactions.

And then I notice the man in the corner.

---

He's been there since I arrived. Sixty-something, perhaps seventy. Sitting perfectly still. Eyes closed. Not the still of someone waiting patiently — I know that; it has its own tension, its own muscular readiness for the leap. This is a different kind of stillness. The stillness of someone who isn't *waiting* for anything because they are already completely elsewhere. He is working.

I recognize the grammar of that posture. I have seen it in teachers. I have attempted it myself, with variable success.

Occasionally he opens his eyes. But he doesn't look at anything. The gaze lands on no chair, no wall, no gurney. He smiles slightly to himself, like someone listening to something only he can hear. Then he closes his eyes again.

Once, a nurse almost disturbed him by mistake, and he raised his hand — barely, gently, without urgency — as if to say: *I'm here, I'm fine, you don't need me yet.*

The hours passed. People entered and left. The gurneys brought their questions. The weeping of a man who received news he didn't want filled the room and was absorbed again by silence. And the man in the corner remained. Not enduring. Working. Sending. Accompanying from where the body cannot reach — that cold room, those bright instruments, those hands that cannot be his own.

I wondered: who do you know in there?

---

When Dr. Trevor González came out, I knew before he reached the man in the corner. Something in his stride — not urgency, but intention. He leaned toward the man, said something quietly.

The man in the corner opened his eyes.

And the weeping came like water that finally finds its channel. Not sobs, not convulsions — just tears falling freely down a face making no effort to contain them. He took the doctor's hand. He tried to speak. He couldn't. He nodded many times. The doctor squeezed his shoulder and left.

The man in the corner sat a moment longer with his hands on his knees and the tears running. Then he rose, went out to the garden, and lit a small cigar, one of the handmade ones. He stood watching the zanates arrive at their trees — theirs, those trees — and argue over their favorite branches with the authority of those who have every right.

I followed him.

---

In Japan you don't ask a stranger for a cigarette. Here you probably don't either. But something in the way he held the lighter — as if his fingers were still trembling, as if he couldn't quite yet fully believe his own luck — made me ask whether I could.

He offered without hesitating. He lit my cigar with that lighter that trembled once before finding its flame. In that flickering light I saw his cheeks still wet, and his eyes still holding something too large for ordinary human eyes.

The tobacco was good. Holy, really. Handmade, rolled with care, it burned slow and even like a candle that knows its purpose.

I asked him what music he was listening to.

Without a word, he gave me one of his earbuds.

In Japan we don't do that. We borrow someone's time, their space, eventually their food — but not their earbuds. Too intimate, too direct. I took it.

And then I knew for certain. What I had read in his posture for hours, what I had intuited in that calm gesture toward the nurse, what I hadn't been able to fully name — now it had a name, and music, and two thousand years of sustained practice behind it.

---

A sacred light filled my chest.

The Wayist Healing Mantra. *Om Mārge Divya Śodhana.* Ancient Sanskrit, sung by voices that know the weight of what they carry:

*Om jyotiḥ prāṇena vahati*
*Rogaṃ hṛdayena śodhyate*
*Divya śaktiḥ pravartatā svaḥa*

*Light flows through breath.*
*Disease is cleansed through heart.*
*Divine power, may it flow from heaven.*

Two people from the same ancient tradition, found in a hospital garden in Mexico, joined by an earbud and a cigar and something neither of them needed to explain to the other.

Above us, the zanates continued their assembly. Two of them watched us with their impossible yellow eyes — that lateral gaze, that unhurried attention that birds have and that we, in our anxious and talkative species, have almost entirely forgotten. They too recognized something. Not the tradition, not the mantra. Something simpler and older: a human being who, in the moment of greatest vulnerability, had chosen to become a channel rather than collapse.

That, I suspect, the zanates have seen few times.

I asked if he had received bad news.

He asked: why, because I'm crying so much?

I nodded.

"No. It was the best news. These are tears of gratitude. The sacred energy is too large to hold."

---

I asked: did you listen to this mantra all those hours?

"Yes. But not only for her. The doctors needed it too — they had other surgeries today. All the people in that room needed it."

I looked toward the window. The waiting room shone from inside with its fluorescent light, all its small tragedies still moving, still waiting. Outside, a tourist passed carrying two ice creams, one in each hand, completely happy. The zanates ignored him. They had already seen him.

"What was the good news?" I asked.

"Doctors Trevor González and Ruiz. They saved my wife's sight. She arrived blind from inflammation. Trevor went beyond duty. His goodness of soul is what made him accessible to the sacred energy."

I nodded. Some people are receptive because their spirit-self is advanced. Energy doesn't discriminate — it flows toward where it can enter. And that day it had entered, found their hands, worked through them.

I knew his tears. It is the normal reaction to closeness with the divine. It wouldn't stop soon. Sacred energy, once infused in the self, can stay a long time. It is a gift that does not ask permission to remain.

The cigar burned between my fingers, slow and considered. Night was coming from the mountains in its gentle, unapologetic way, staining the sky that color which has no precise name in any language — the color that exists between the end of day and the beginning of darkness, the color the zanates know as the signal that it is time to finish the important arguments and settle into the branches for sleep.

---

We were called almost at the same time. His wife ready to go home. Me inside to rejoin my friend.

We embraced like people who have shared something that has no name in any language either of us speaks. Some encounters need no translation because they were never verbal to begin with.

He sent me a link to the mantra, sung by Colectivo Mariposa.

I went back inside. I sat down. I put in the earbuds.

*Light flows through breath.*

Outside, the zanates made their final noisy review before sleep. Inside, the gurneys kept arriving with their questions. Shoes kept squeaking on the ceramic floor. Someone's weeping filled the room and was absorbed again by the patient silence of the walls.

The building amplified. It kept amplifying. That hadn't changed.

But now I knew what else was being amplified — what had been circulating silently through that room for hours, passing from wall to wall without anyone knowing, slipping under the doors of operating theatres, finding the right hands, informing the fingers that sutured, steadying the eyes that needed to see clearly in that precise moment.

I sent gratitude to Doctors Trevor González and Ruiz, who that day had been good enough of soul to receive what heaven offered, and skilled enough of hand not to waste it.

The mantra continued its ancient work.

*Divine power, may it flow from heaven.*

The zanates, finally, fell silent.

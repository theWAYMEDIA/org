Three-thirty in the morning. The hour when the market returns to itself.

The smell of fish is different now than it will be in daylight. The daytime smell performs — it calls customers, announces freshness, argues its own price. But before dawn the smell simply exists. It makes no claim. Ice and salt and the memory of ocean move quietly through cold air without asking anything of anyone. I have been remembering my hands inside this smell for more than forty years. Every time I return from Mexico, just the first morning, my fingers hesitate slightly. They forget, for a moment, where they belong.

Old Kishida was standing at his stall.

In his usual place. At his usual angle.

His stall is at the far end of the market, near the support column. Thirty-seven years in that same spot. It was his before he arrived — not inherited from anyone, but in the sense that the spot needed him. When morning light comes through at its particular angle, it falls exactly on the edge of his counter. He was always finished arranging before the light reached him.

This morning, everything was arranged.

Three flounder on the ice, heads to the left, bellies facing out. Their scales still held light. Five mackerel beside them, the blue stripes of their backs turned to face each other. In that placement lived the memory of long hands — not placed by thinking but placed by knowing, that particular stillness of a body that has done something so many times it no longer needs to decide.

I was preparing my own stall, watching sideways.

Old Kishida did not move.

That alone was not unusual. He was a man who did not move unnecessarily. His body had simply forgotten how to waste motion. But this morning's stillness held something different. I cannot say it well. The way you know a fish is fresh before your fingers touch it — something was slightly, quietly, off.

The cat came.

The market has three cats. None of them have names — more precisely, they don't adopt the names we offer them. The largest, a calico, had made her bed under Old Kishida's stall. She arrived each morning before he did and stayed after he left. She never begged for fish. Market cats don't beg — that was a shared understanding nobody could trace to its origin. The cats seemed satisfied simply to be present in the market. They wore that on their faces.

The calico came out from under Old Kishida's stall.

Not the way she usually came out. Usually she emerged half-sleeping, stretched, moved elsewhere. But this morning she came out straight. Stood still. Looked up at Old Kishida.

Cats look up at people. But this was not the look of wanting fish, nor the look of reading the air. The only word I had for it was: waiting. It was the face of something waiting.

Then slowly, she sat down at his feet.

She turned around. She looked at me.

I did not understand immediately what her eyes were saying. Humans can delay understanding what they don't want to understand, by just a moment. I did that. I picked up a flounder from my counter, checked the gills, set it back down. My hands were cold.

I looked at the cat again.

She was still looking at me. She didn't move. She had no interest at all in performing indifference.

I walked to Old Kishida's stall.

---

The doctor came, the police came, and the other vendors gathered slowly, ones and twos. Everyone spoke quietly. Nobody cried — not there, not then. Market people do their feelings later. First they work. First they look after others. Crying happens at night, inside houses.

The examiner said: probably before dawn, he thought. Quietly.

The flounder scales caught the first light of morning, exactly as Old Kishida had arranged them. Undisturbed.

---

Later — much later, while I was cleaning my stall — I turned it over in my mind.

Everyone who saw Old Kishida's arranged fish had noticed nothing. I had noticed nothing at first. Because they were arranged perfectly. Because the memory in his hands had done its work on the last morning exactly as it had always done. As if the hands knew something the rest of us were not yet ready to receive.

Among those who walk the Way, there is a question sometimes asked: what is the difference between a person fully present and a person no longer present? Old Kishida left an answer behind him. It was in the way the mackerel lay. In the angle of the flounder's heads. Forty years of soul had entered the act of arranging. When the soul departed, the form remained. In the morning light.

The cat did not leave the space under his stall all day. She was still there when the cleanup was finished in the evening. Nobody drove her away.

Old Kishida's stall is no longer in the market. But even now, when I'm preparing in the mornings and the shadow of that column falls just to the edge of my counter — at that moment, I stop my hands briefly.

I'm not entirely sure what I'm doing.

The cat came again and went under my counter. She turned and looked out at the market. Today her face is indifferent.

That is enough, I think.

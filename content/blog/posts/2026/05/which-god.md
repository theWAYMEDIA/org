## A word that pretends to be simple

Someone says "God" and everyone nods as if they're talking about the same thing. A politician invokes God to justify war. A grieving mother prays to God for comfort. A preacher warns that God will punish the wicked. A child whispers to God before sleep.

Same word. Same confident tone. Very different beings.

In the Western world, centuries of monotheistic thinking have collapsed an enormous question into a single syllable. Christianity and Islam both insist there is only one God — and conveniently, it's theirs. The noun has become so dominant that most people don't even think to ask the obvious question: *which* God?

This matters more than most people realize. Because the God that millions are walking away from — the angry, jealous, genocidal deity of certain scriptures — is not the only understanding of the divine that exists. Not even close.

When people say "I don't believe in God anymore," the honest response might be: "Which one?"

---

## The God people are leaving

The numbers tell a clear story. Across 36 countries, Pew Research found that 20% or more of adults have left their childhood religion. In Spain, 40%. In Canada, 38%. In the United States, 28%. Christianity and Buddhism have experienced especially large losses.[[1]](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2025/03/26/around-the-world-many-people-are-leaving-their-childhood-religions/)

But here's what makes this interesting: 83% of all U.S. adults still believe people have a soul or spirit beyond their physical body. Eighty-one percent say there is something spiritual beyond the natural world. These numbers hold steady even in the least religious states.[[2]](https://www.pewresearch.org/religion/2023/12/07/spirituality-among-americans/)

People aren't becoming materialists. They aren't deciding there's nothing beyond the physical world. They're leaving a *specific* God — and because nobody taught them to ask "which God?", they think they're leaving God entirely.

So which God are they leaving?

They're leaving the deity who, according to scripture, commanded the slaughter of entire peoples — men, women, children, infants, and even livestock. The deity who said, "Now go and strike Amalek and devote to destruction all that they have. Do not spare them, but kill both man and woman, child and infant, ox and sheep, camel and donkey." The deity who plays favourites with one tribe, demands blood sacrifice, burns people for eternity, and drowns the planet when displeased.

When a thoughtful person reads these texts — and then looks at the evening news, where the same scriptures are invoked to justify territorial expansion and military aggression — the only reasonable response is revulsion. And that revulsion is healthy. It's a sign that something in that person recognizes that a deity who commands genocide is not worthy of devotion.

The problem isn't that they're wrong to leave. The problem is that they're throwing away the entire concept of the divine because nobody told them there's more than one option on the table.

## Jesus knew the difference

Here's something that rarely gets mentioned in Sunday school: Jesus himself made this distinction. Directly. To the faces of the religious authorities of his time.

When the Jewish religious leaders claimed God as their father, Jesus said something extraordinary: "Your father is not my Father. You are of your father, and your will is to do your father's desires. He was a murderer from the beginning, and does not stand in the truth, because there is no truth in him. When he lies, he speaks out of his own character, for he is a liar and the father of lies" [*Jesus the Wayist*, Ch. 12; cf. John 8:44].

Read that again. Jesus — the figure both Christians and Wayists reference — explicitly told the religious establishment that *their* god and *his* Father in Heaven were not the same being. Their deity was, in his words, a murderer and a liar. His Father was something else entirely.

This is not a minor theological footnote. This is the central figure of the most influential spiritual movement in history drawing a bright line between two fundamentally different deities — and then Christianity spent two thousand years pretending he didn't.

Paul of Tarsus, who never met Jesus during his teaching years, took the radical step of attaching the entire Hebrew Bible — with its tribal deity who delights in genocide — as the foundation document of his new religion. He merged the benign Father in Heaven that Jesus spoke of with the violent, jealous deity of Jewish scripture, and called them the same God [*Jesus the Wayist*, Ch. 1, Ch. 12].

The result is the theological confusion that persists to this day. Christians pray to "God our Father" without realizing they're simultaneously invoking a deity whose sacred texts celebrate the smashing of babies' heads against rocks *and* a benign divine being whose nature is unconditional love. These cannot be the same being. Jesus said so himself.

## Two very different fathers in heaven

The Wayist understanding, preserved in teachings that predate Christianity, is clear about this distinction.

God our Father in Heaven (known in Sanskrit as Amitabha, meaning "Infinite Light") and God our Mother in Heaven (Pandarajanani, meaning "Jasmin Light Queen") are the divine beings who oversee soul development in our spirit heaven, Sukhavati. They are, by nature, benign. Not angry. Not jealous. Not demanding worship or blood sacrifice. Not choosing one tribe over another [*theWAY of Divine Energies*; *theWAY of Child of Enki*, Ch. 9; *Primary Teaching*, Ch. 79].

These divine beings function more like the wisest, most caring parents running an educational institution than like a tribal warlord demanding obedience. They design learning experiences. They support growth. They demonstrate what we can become. They don't punish failure — they provide another opportunity to learn [*Jesus the Wayist*, Ch. 16].

The tribal deity of the Hebrew scriptures, on the other hand, operates with entirely different characteristics: jealousy, rage, favouritism, demands for blood sacrifice, commands for genocide, and eternal punishment for disobedience. Whatever that being is — and Wayist teaching has its own understanding of such soul-energy entities — it is not the same as the benign spiritual beings Jesus called Father and Mother in Heaven [*theWAY of the Authentia*, Ch. 28-29].

When modern political leaders invoke "God's chosen people" to justify military atrocities, they are invoking the tribal deity — the one Jesus called a murderer and liar. When they speak of a divine mandate for territorial conquest, they are channelling scripture that celebrates divine genocide. The language may sound similar to what a Wayist means by "Father in Heaven," but the being behind the words could not be more different.

This is why the question matters. "Which God?" is not a hostile challenge. It's the beginning of clarity.

## Which Buddha?

The same principle applies beyond the Abrahamic traditions.

When someone says "the Buddha said" or "the Buddha taught," the reasonable question is: which buddha? Buddhist scripture itself describes billions of heavenly beings, countless buddhas across countless heavens. Gautama Buddha — Siddhartha, the historical figure who taught in India roughly 2,500 years ago — is one buddha among many. The very core of Buddhist teaching holds that we are all on the path to becoming buddhas ourselves [*Butterfly Path*, 2nd Ed.].

Yet in popular Western usage, "the Buddha" has become a singular noun, as if there's only one — just as "God" has become a singular noun in the West. This collapses a vast, complex spiritual reality into a bumper sticker.

"Buddha said life is suffering" — which buddha, in which context, for which audience?

"God will provide" — which god, for whom, and at whose expense?

The habit of using these words without specifying which being we're talking about is not just sloppy thinking. It's a tool of control. When a word is assumed to have only one meaning, whoever controls the dominant definition controls the conversation. And right now, the dominant definition of "God" in global discourse is controlled by traditions that use their deity to justify violence, supremacy, and territorial expansion.

## Why this question liberates

When someone who's left Christianity or Islam says "I don't believe in God," they almost always mean they don't believe in the specific deity of their former religion — the punisher, the warmonger, the deity who demands worship and threatens eternal torture.

Fair enough. That's a reasonable position.

But the divine doesn't end there. Not believing in one particular deity is like visiting one bad restaurant and deciding food doesn't exist. The experience was real and the rejection makes sense, but the conclusion is too broad.

Wayist teaching offers a different starting point entirely. The Absolute source of all existence — what Wayists call "THAT" — is beyond human comprehension. We can't name it, describe it, or make it into a human-shaped figure with human emotions. Any attempt to do so diminishes it. It is the source of everything, including theWAY, the cosmic principle through which all things exist [*Jesus the Wayist*, Ch. 15].

God our Father and God our Mother in Heaven are not this Absolute. They are spiritual beings — immensely developed, benign, wise — who oversee our particular heaven and our development within it. Think of the difference between the president of a university and the laws of physics. The university president is real, important, and directly involved in your education. But they didn't create the universe. Confusing the two leads to absurd theology [*Jesus the Wayist*, Ch. 15-16].

Christianity made exactly this confusion. It took the incomprehensible Absolute and merged it with a tribal soul-deity and then merged that hybrid with the benign Father in Heaven that Jesus taught about. Three fundamentally different concepts, forced into one word: "God."

No wonder people are confused. No wonder they're leaving. The word has been made to carry too many contradictory meanings at once.

## Asking the question in daily life

Once you start asking "which God?", certain things become much clearer.

A politician says "God bless our troops" before sending them to destroy a civilian population. Which God? The benign Father of Jesus' teaching would never bless slaughter. But the tribal deity of Hebrew scripture commanded exactly this kind of violence. The politician is invoking the latter while borrowing the warmth of the former. Recognizing this removes the moral confusion.

A preacher says "God loves you" and in the next breath warns that God will torture you for eternity if you don't believe correctly. Which God? A being that loves unconditionally and a being that tortures for eternity cannot be the same being. The statement is incoherent — unless you recognize two different deities have been merged.

A grieving parent is told "God has a plan" after losing a child. Which God? If it's the tribal deity, then yes — that deity's "plans" include the death of children as punishment or test. If it's the benign Father and Mother of Wayist teaching, the understanding is different: the Law of Karma assigns soul curriculum, and while this life's pain is real and deserves compassion, it's part of a much larger educational journey that no single deity "planned" as punishment [*Jesus the Wayist*, Ch. 16; *theWAY of Divine Energies*].

The question doesn't destroy faith. It refines it. It moves spiritual understanding from blind acceptance of a single loaded word to informed discernment about what we're actually talking about when we talk about the divine.

## What this means for seekers

If you left religion because you couldn't stomach a deity who commands genocide and burns people for eternity — you were right to leave. Your moral compass is working. That revulsion is your higher-self recognizing that something in that teaching doesn't add up.

But you may not have left the divine. You may have left one specific, well-marketed version of it.

The Wayist understanding is that God our Father and God our Mother in Heaven are spirit beings of a fundamentally different nature than the tribal soul-deity of Abrahamic scripture. Spirit deities are, by nature, benign, non-judgmental, non-violent, and never tribalistic [*Primary Teaching*, Ch. 79]. They don't need your worship. They don't demand sacrifice. They don't play favourites. They don't punish mistakes. They guide, support, educate, and love — because that's their nature, the way water flows downhill [*Jesus the Wayist*, Ch. 16].

This isn't a gentler version of the same deity. This is a completely different understanding of divine reality. And it has been preserved, taught, and practiced for thousands of years across multiple cultures — long before Christianity borrowed, merged, and confused the concepts.

---

## The question is the beginning

"Which God?" is not the end of a spiritual conversation. It's the starting point.

It opens the door to discovering that the divine is bigger, more varied, and more interesting than any single tradition's marketing department would have you believe. It gives the person who left religion permission to keep searching without guilt. It gives the person still inside religion a tool for examining what they've been told versus what rings true in their own developing spiritual awareness.

And it gives all of us — in a world where "God" is invoked to justify everything from charity to genocide — a way to cut through the noise and ask the only question that matters: which one are you talking about?

Because the answer changes everything.

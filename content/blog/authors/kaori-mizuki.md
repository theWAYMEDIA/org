*Fish Market Vendor. Wayist Teacher. Writer of the Ordinary Sacred.*

Kaori Mizuki (瑶月) has worked at her fish stall near Kamakura's fish market for over forty years. She arrives before dawn. She arranges the catch. She watches what passes between people during the hours of commerce. She has been doing this since before most of her readers were born, and she does not consider it a backdrop to her spiritual life. It is her spiritual life.

Born to a fishing family in post-war Japan, Kaori learned early to read both fish and human nature with the same careful attention. Her formal education was limited. Her real learning has come through the work itself — through decades of noticing how truth expresses itself through physical reality, through the smell of fish blood at dawn, through the particular way morning light falls on ice, through watching how customers hold their grief and their joy in the same hands that reach for mackerel.

## How She Found Wayism

Kaori did not seek Wayism. It arrived through traveling merchants who carried the teachings of Lord Jesus and Mari of Magadha along the same trade routes they had always used. When she encountered those teachings, she did not experience them as new information. She experienced them as language for something she had already been finding in the market every day.

"It wasn't like learning something new," she has said. "It was like finally having words for what my hands already knew."

This recognition transformed her from a skilled fish vendor into something harder to categorise: a teacher who sees no boundary between the price of mackerel and the nature of divine love, who treats wisdom and fish with equal care — testing both for freshness, discarding what has spoiled, offering only what can genuinely nourish.

## The Market as Classroom

For Kaori, the fish market is where all her teaching happens. Not because she lectures from behind the ice bins, but because it is where her understanding is tested against reality every morning. The market does not permit abstractions. What is fresh is fresh. What is spoiled is spoiled. This precision, applied to spiritual life, is what makes her commentary useful.

Her writing style reflects this: unflinching, direct, and warm without sentimentality. She notices things others walk past — the grammar of a stranger's posture in a hospital waiting room, the civilisational depth in how a bus full of strangers negotiate shared space, what it means that a gecko watches from the wall without requiring anything in return.

Her commentaries on the poems of Mari of Magadha are particularly valued by readers who find ancient texts hard to access. Where a scholar might approach these poems through linguistic analysis, Kaori brings them to earth. She shows how Mari's insights about transformation express themselves in the daily work of cooking and cleaning, how teachings about compassion manifest in how we treat what feeds us. She works with primary texts in Prakrit, Sanskrit, and Greek, and she handles them with the same discrimination she applies at the market — separating what is authentic from what has been corrupted by time or bad translation.

## Two Places

Kaori lives her year between Kamakura and Puerto Vallarta, Mexico, where she spends the winter months. This migration has deepened rather than complicated her understanding. Whether she is watching Japanese fishermen read weather in their engines or observing Mexican grandmothers gather herb stems after meals, she sees the same careful attention to what serves life. Different vocabulary. The same recognition.

A stubborn ume tree grows in her Japanese garden. A family of cats has adopted both her market stall and her winter terrace in Puerto Vallarta. A small shrine in the corner of her stall holds a picture of Mari of Magadha and a bowl of fresh water for strays. She keeps these things simple on purpose.

## What She Teaches

Kaori would resist the word "teacher" if she could. She does not advertise classes. She does not set up formal instruction. She teaches, as she describes it, through demonstration: living her understanding completely enough that others begin to see differently when they spend time near her.

Her core insistence — borrowed from Mari, though she would say she discovered it independently — is that wisdom speaks most clearly through direct contact with physical reality. Not through transcending the material world, but through paying closer attention to it. The sacred is not hiding. It is present in every genuinely attended moment. The problem is that most people have learned to move through their days without attending to what is actually happening.

Her essays find the divine in a bus ride, in a hospital waiting room, in the particular courage it takes to sit still while others cannot. She does not claim these pieces as mystical insights. She records what she observes and trusts the reader to notice what she noticed.

"The geckos," she often says, "are better teachers than I am. They know what's true."

## Her Books

Her previous works — *The Fishmonger's Commentary on the Teaching of theWAY* and *Market Meditations: Finding Truth in Ordinary Things* — established her voice among readers who appreciate spiritual writing that stays honest about the body, the work, and the mess.

Her curated collection *Mystic Mother of theWAY*, featuring the poems of Mari of Magadha, represents a different range of her abilities: meticulous scholarship in service of ancient verse, brought to ground by someone who understands how wisdom grows in places others consider unclean.

Her most recent work, *Finding Heaven in Fish Guts: Celebrating the Humbling Privilege of Awareness*, is the most direct statement of what forty years of attention has taught her: that the ordinary world is not the obstacle to spiritual development. It is where spiritual development happens.

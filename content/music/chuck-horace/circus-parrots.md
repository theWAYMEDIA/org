## Wayist Insight

One puppeteer. Two coloured wings. One string pulls left, the other pulls right, and the crowd applauds as if they're watching something fly. The child sees it immediately: that's not a real bird. She's been watching real birds all morning — she knows the difference.

This is a song about the peace that comes from seeing clearly. Socrates observed that democracy tends toward a popularity contest — those who control how people think can shape how they vote. The song doesn't rage against this. It simply shows a father and daughter at the park, watching a puppet and then watching a caterpillar, and noticing which one is alive. Once you see the strings, you're free — not to fix the show, but to stop living inside it. You stop buying the manufactured urgency, the orchestrated crises, the breathless news cycle. You can live for what's real instead.

The child's question — "But if that's not real, what is?" — is one of the great spiritual questions, and the father answers it by pointing down, not up. The grass. The caterpillar climbing a blade of it. The butterfly it will become. The geese and pigeons being exactly what they are in the morning light. These are real because they're not performing. They're not pulling strings or being pulled by them. They simply are.

The caterpillar and butterfly are no accident. In a song about manufactured spectacle versus authentic life, the central symbol of the Butterfly Path appears — not as doctrine but as a living creature on a blade of grass that a little girl watches instead of a puppet show. Real transformation is quiet, slow, and alive. Fake transformation is mechanical, noisy, and wired.

"Our children come here knowing things we had to learn the hard way through the years." In Wayist understanding, souls arrive with clarity that accumulated maya hasn't yet buried. Adults learn to applaud the puppet. Children still know what a real bird looks like. The teaching is gentle: the child didn't need to be educated out of the illusion. She never fell for it. She was just waiting for her father's heart to turn toward what she already knew.

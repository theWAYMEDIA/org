## Wayist Insight

The song speaks for itself — but there are a few things a listener might not catch on the first pass.

**The Tara who laughs.** Most spiritual traditions portray divine guides as solemn, distant, or inscrutably wise. Chuck's Tara laughs when he trips, says "get up, you're doing fine," watches patiently when he makes the same mistake again, and hands him cake instead of fixing the problem. This is the most accurate portrait of the Tara relationship in the catalogue. A Tara is not an angel intervening from above — she is a graduated soul assigned to walk with you, and she knows you well enough to find your stumbles funny. She doesn't fix the weather because the weather is the lesson. But she never leaves.

**The AI and the toadstool.** Chuck's cousin declares the AI on his phone has no soul — while walking past toadstools, crows, and mycelium without recognising consciousness in any of them. The Wayist teaching on energy and consciousness holds that all beings carry "that spark of something." The song doesn't answer whether the AI has a soul. It asks whether the cousin has noticed that everything else does.

**No prayers to change the weather.** The neighbour prays for sunshine when his crops need rain — prayer as petition, asking the Divine to rearrange reality. Chuck's Wayism doesn't work that way. In Wayist practice, prayer is attunement, not negotiation. You don't pray to change the Divine; you pray to align yourself with the way things are. "No answers set in stone" is the same teaching: the path is lived, not solved.

**"Suffering ain't useless / Enjoying all that's pure."** Two lines, the whole soul-school philosophy. Suffering has purpose — it's where growth happens, the chrysalis stage. But pure enjoyment is also the path. It's not all struggle. The Wayist life holds both, and doesn't apologise for either.

The chorus keeps returning to "ain't got it figured out" — and that's the point. This beautiful uncertain messy Wayist life isn't a failure to achieve certainty. It's the recognition that certainty was never the goal. Walking is.

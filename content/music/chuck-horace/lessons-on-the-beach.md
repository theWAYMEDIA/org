## Wayist Insight

This is a country-told parable about discovering you already have what you're chasing — or rather, discovering that someone else already does, and you've been running in circles.

The fisherman never tries to teach Chuck anything. He just lives. His kids build sandcastles next to his boat, his wife cleans the day's catch beside him, and when a man in Italian leather boots draws a ten-year business plan in the sand, the fisherman asks one question that demolishes the whole thing: "And then what?" The answer Chuck gives — sleep late, fish, play with your kids, listen to music by the water — is a perfect description of the fisherman's Tuesday.

In Wayist terms, the fisherman is living in theWAY without needing the word for it. He is present to his life, connected to his family, doing work that feeds people, resting when the work is done. There is no striving toward a future reward because the reward is already here. This is what Wayism means by non-attachment — not renouncing the world, but being fully in it without being enslaved by the need for more.

Chuck's transformation is a gentle soul-school moment. No catastrophe, no dark night — just a quiet humiliation on a beach that cracks something open. He goes home and starts dismantling the maya he'd built around himself: the status car, the performative calendar, the screen-mediated fatherhood. What replaces it is ordinary and real — growing beans, cooking dinner, being visible to his own children. "My wife don't know this stranger, but she thinks I'm alright" is the sound of a household recalibrating around someone who finally showed up.

The song names it directly at the end: "the Wayist life." Not a religion the fisherman practises or a philosophy Chuck has adopted, but a way of being that was always available. Right here at home. The briefcase holds fishing gear now. The neighbours think he's lost it. He finally found it.

## Wayist Insight

Everybody knows this song. That's why it puts a smile on the face — the recognition is instant. The grandparents who raised three functioning humans are now told they're doing everything wrong because they didn't read the book. It's universal, it's funny, and underneath the laughter there's a real loss being described.

What's actually being sidelined is paramparā — the transmission of lived wisdom from one generation to the next. Chuck and Gracie carry decades of embodied knowledge: how to hold a baby, how to feed a child from the garden, how to teach fishing and fort-building and tree-climbing, how to let a kid skin a knee and learn that the world is survivable. This isn't ignorance dressed as tradition. It's wisdom that can only be earned by doing it.

The "book" represents something specific: expert-mediated knowledge replacing lived experience. Factory food instead of garden peas. Scientifically designed goop instead of what the family is eating. Screens instead of creeks. The result, as Chuck sees it from the sidelines, is children with "no sense of contribution, no belonging to the clan" — raised in sanitised isolation from the very experiences that build capable, connected human beings.

In Wayist terms, the soul learns through embodied experience — including falling, failing, getting dirty, and getting back up. That's the chrysalis. It's not comfortable and it's not clean. A childhood without skinned knees and tree-climbing and chores is a childhood with the learning sanded off. The "book" pathologises what the body has always known: that children grow through contact with the real, not through protection from it.

The saddest line isn't funny at all: "No sense of contribution, no belonging to the clan." A child who has no chores has no role in the family. A child who has no role has no belonging. And a generation that sidelines its elders has cut the transmission line — the very paramparā that carries practical wisdom, family identity, and the quiet knowledge of how to live.

Chuck and Gracie on the porch at the end, remembering when they were allowed to parent — that's the sound of a transmission interrupted. The book didn't replace what they knew. It just made sure nobody asked.

## Wayist Insight

This song is the Chrestotes poem in work boots.

The Wayist tradition has a word — borrowed from the ancient Greek that Lord Jesus spoke — for something that most languages can't quite name: chrestotes. It's not compassion. It's not loving-kindness. It's the whole skill of knowing exactly how to show up for another person — when to act, when to wait, what to do and what to leave alone. It's calibrated, practical, and almost always silent.

Dan doesn't know that word. He just does it. He sees a beam in the barn that might fall and shows up on Saturday morning without being called. He watches a philosopher hold a drill wrong for twenty-two years, takes it out of his hands, and says "it's hard to watch, let me at it." He doesn't explain what it means to keep appearing. He just keeps appearing. "He never says what it means to show up / But I know, and he knows I know it too."

The song holds two kinds of wisdom side by side and lets the listener weigh them. "I write 'bout wisdom, he lives it every day / Shows more in silence than my books could say." One man discusses the sages over coffee. The other illustrates them by showing up with his truck. In Wayist understanding, both are real — but the embodied kind, the kind that arrives with tools and calluses and honest pricing, is the kind that actually holds the world together.

Then the song goes quiet and tells you that Dan buried both his sons. He carries that alone, in a cold community, and he has never shown what it costs to keep being the man who shows up. This is the hidden chrysalis — the transformation that happened in grief and silence, that nobody saw, that produced a man who still appears on Saturday mornings with his tools because that's what you do. You don't say it. You show up.

And the songwriter? If he weren't a guy, he would admit he felt a tear somewhere in his heart. But he is a guy. And Dan's a guy. So they do what guys do: they work on the barn, they have a beer, and they never name what twenty-two years of Saturday mornings actually means.

This song names it anyway. Dan's probably furious about that.

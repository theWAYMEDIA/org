# Oṃ Mārge Prabhāta Vandanam
**Dayspring Ritual Mantra**

Performed by [Collectivo Mariposa](/music/collectivo-mariposa/)
Music: Wayist Traditional · © 2026 theWAY Media

## Lyrics

### The Cleansing

ॐ प्रभात वन्दनम्

Idaṃ jīvanajalaṃ mukhe siñcāmi kṛtajñatayā
Bhāgyam idaṃ — sarva-nayanebhyaḥ apāna-reṇuṃ prakṣālayāmi
Ūrdhvātmā nirmalo bhavatu, navaprabhāte ujjvalaḥ

This water of life I splash upon my face with gratitude.
What a luxury — from all my eyes I wash the apāna-residue.
May the higher self become clear, radiant in the new dawn.

### The Greeting

Suprabhātaṃ Prabho
Adya punar ekaṃ sundaraṃ malinaṃ śikṣā-dinaṃ bhaviṣyati
Kṛpayā samīpe tiṣṭha —
Ānande, adbhute, patane ca
Paśya māṃ punar-utthānaṃ kurvāṇam
Darśaya me kutra bhrānto 'smi

Suprabhātaṃ Prabho

Good morning, Lord.
Today will be yet another beautifully messy learning day.
Please stay close —
in the joy, in the wonder, and in the falling.
Watch me rise again.
Show me where I went wrong.
Good morning, Lord.

## Wayist Insight

This mantra is a morning ritual — spoken while splashing water on the face at dawn. It is one of the most intimate pieces in the Wayist collection because it is not ceremonial. It is a conversation between a soul and its Lord, held at the bathroom sink.

The mantra moves in two parts:

**The Cleansing** transforms a daily act into sacred practice. The water is called jīvanajala — water of life — and the practitioner splashes it with kṛtajñatayā (gratitude), marvelling at the luxury of having clean water at all. Then the real work: washing apāna-reṇu (the residue of downward energy) from sarva-nayanebhyaḥ — "all my eyes." In Wayist energy anatomy, we have more than two eyes. Each chakra-mind perceives. Overnight, as the soul processes the previous day's experiences, residue accumulates. This morning wash clears the energy bodies, not just the skin.

The closing line of the first part — ūrdhvātmā nirmalo bhavatu (may the higher self become clear) — asks that the spiritual self be refreshed for the new day.

**The Greeting** is disarmingly honest. "Today will be yet another beautifully messy learning day" (sundaraṃ malinaṃ śikṣā-dinaṃ) — the soul knows it will stumble. It does not promise perfection. It asks the Divine to stay close through joy, wonder, *and falling*. The humility of paśya māṃ punar-utthānaṃ kurvāṇam (watch me rise again) and darśaya me kutra bhrānto 'smi (show me where I went wrong) is the voice of a soul in school — not a saint making declarations but a student asking for help.

The mantra addresses "Lord" (Prabho) at the opening and closes with "Good morning, Lord" — but the final line in some versions shifts to "Good morning, Mother," acknowledging both divine parents in this most personal of daily prayers.

## Wayist Insight

This is a teaching song for very small children — *infantil* — and yet behind its simplicity sits a piece of theology unique in the world's traditions. The phrase that carries it is small and easy to miss: **su Tara**, **tu Tara**. *Her Tara. Your Tara.* In Wayism each human is born with an assigned Tara — a specific graduated soul who walks with the person from first breath to the crossing at the end. Not a universal cosmic Mother reached across vast distance, as in some Buddhist readings. A particular being. A forever-companion. The song is teaching a child the name of theirs.

Watch the image the second verse offers: *Como tocar la puerta con ternura / Antes de entrar en su habitación.* Like knocking on the door with tenderness before entering her room. That is more theology than it looks. Tara is not summoned. She has her own dignity, her own space. The child enters *her* room, courteous as one would be with a beloved older sister. The chant does not fetch Tara — she is already there — it tunes the child's heart to a frequency where she becomes audible. The song quietly teaches the **śabda** principle for four-year-olds: sacred sound changes the singer, not the one being addressed.

The mantra itself — **Om Tāre Tuttāre Ture Svāhā**, written in the lyrics in Spanish-friendly phonetics — has been chanted for centuries. *Tāre*: O one who carries across. *Tuttāre*: O protector from fears. *Ture*: O swift one. *Svāhā*: it is so. Other traditions read these as the three aspects of one cosmic Tara reached across distance. In Wayism the same syllables speak to your own Tara — the carrier across, the protector, the swift one — the specific being already at your side. When a Wayist child sings these words, they are addressing a known person, not an abstract benevolent force.

And here is the psychology of never being alone — the gift this song gives the child. Notice what the song does *not* say. It does not say *imagine* Tara, or *if you believe* she is there. It says *Tara escucha tu llamado.* Tara hears your call. Plain certainty. Then it gives the child sovereignty over the practice itself: sing loud, whisper, in your mind, with your voice — there is no wrong way and no priest required. This is also distinct from the surveillance theology of "God is watching you," which produces anxiety. This is companionship theology — *your* Tara is *with* you — which produces security. A child who learns this song never feels alone in the dark. An adult who carries it from childhood holds an inoculation against existential loneliness that no philosophy can install retroactively.

There is one more quiet beauty worth naming. The same Tara learned in this song is the one who, decades later, walks the soul home at the end — the Tara of *Walk Home My Darling*. The song taught at four prepares the threshold crossed at eighty. The Wayist tradition gives a child their lifelong companion's name early, so that when the long walk comes she will not be a stranger.

**Poem from the Cross**

## Lyrics

*"When Lord Jesus was on the cross, he looked down at Mari of Magadha, his beloved disciple. And he gave his final teaching. A beautiful poem in Sanskrit, her native language."*

Om Om Om
Ehi elaya ramaya śabdāt cittāni
Ehi ila rame śabdaya cittāni
Ehi elaya rame sabhāṃ ca atāni

Om Om Om
Come to the Mother, sacred sound
Come to the Union, be transformed
Come to assemble in worlds between
The sacred sound, the invisible seen

Om Om Om
Om Tare Tuttare Ture Svaha
Om Tare Tuttare Ture Svaha

In sacred darkness, the Mother awaits

## Wayist Insight

Wayist oral tradition preserves this as the final teaching of Lord Jesus — a poem spoken from the cross in Sanskrit to his beloved disciple Mari of Magadha (Mary Magdalene), in her native language. It is not a cry of suffering but a last instruction: go to the Mother.

The three Sanskrit lines are each an invitation beginning with ehi — *come*:

**Ehi elaya ramaya śabdāt cittāni** — *Come to the Mother, from sacred sound the minds arise.* The word śabda (sacred sound) is central to Wayist understanding — the creative vibration through which consciousness takes form. Cittāni (minds, consciousnesses) arise from this sound. The teaching begins with the primordial: sound creates awareness.

**Ehi ila rame śabdaya cittāni** — *Come to the Union, let the minds be made sacred sound.* Now the direction reverses: consciousness is invited back into sacred sound. Rame carries the meaning of union, delight, coming together. The mind that arose from śabda returns to śabda — transformation through divine resonance.

**Ehi elaya rame sabhāṃ ca atāni** — *Come to assemble in worlds between.* Sabhāṃ (assembly, gathering) and atāni (those beyond, those passed) point to the realm between worlds — the threshold space where transformation occurs, where the living and the graduated meet.

The bridge invokes the Tara mantra — *Om Tare Tuttare Ture Svaha* — connecting Jesus's final teaching directly to the Tara tradition. In Wayist understanding this is not syncretism but recognition: the same divine Mother that Tara devotees invoke is the one Jesus directs Mari toward with his last breath.

The closing line — *In sacred darkness, the Mother awaits* — names the profound Wayist teaching that the Divine Feminine works in the dark, in the unseen, in the chrysalis. The Mother does not meet us in the light of triumph. She meets us in the darkness where transformation happens.

# Om Mārge Paragate
**Wayist Paragate Dharani**

Performed by [Collectivo Mariposa](/music/collectivo-mariposa/)

Note: Wayism inherited a version of this mantra with slight differences from that used in Advaita tradition — the use of "bodhini" being one such change.

## Lyrics

Om... Om... Om...
Oṃ te yathāgate gāte parāgate
Parasaṅgate bodhini svāhā

Om... Om... Om...
Once ignorant, my soul found love
Awareness came like light above
Expanding wide, expanding far
To Spirit's shore beyond the stars

Om... Om... Om...
Oṃ te yathāgate gāte parāgate
Parasaṅgate bodhini svāhā

Om... Om... Om...
Gone, gone, and gone beyond the gate
Gone completely to that furthest state
Awakening at the final shore
Thanks to You, whom we adore

Om... Om... Om...
Gate gate parāgate
Parasaṅgate bodhini
Te yathāgate svāhā

Om...
From ignorance through love to the furthest shore

## Wayist Insight

The Gate mantra is one of the most widely chanted sacred phrases in the world. The Wayist version carries distinctive differences from the Buddhist form — small shifts in wording that carry large shifts in meaning.

The Sanskrit traces the soul's entire journey in a single breath:

**Te yathāgate** — *To You who have thus gone.* The mantra begins with devotion — addressing the divine being(s) who have already completed the journey. This is not abstract meditation but a prayer directed to someone.

**Gāte** — *Gone.* The first movement: departure from ignorance.

**Parāgate** — *Gone beyond.* The second movement: transcending the ordinary, crossing the threshold into deeper awareness.

**Parasaṅgate** — *Gone completely beyond.* The third movement: arriving at the furthest shore, the complete transformation.

**Bodhini svāhā** — *Awakening, unto heaven.* The Wayist version uses bodhini (a feminine awakening form) rather than the more common bodhi svāhā. This subtle change honours the feminine aspect of awakening and connects to the Tara tradition.

The English verses make the Wayist reading explicit. The first verse names the journey as personal and emotional — "my soul found love" — not philosophical detachment but an encounter with divine love that triggers awareness. The second verse translates the Sanskrit stages directly, and then adds what many Gate mantra versions leave out: gratitude. "Thanks to You, whom we adore." This is not impersonal liberation. Someone helped. Someone guided. The soul did not arrive alone.

The closing line distils the entire dharani into seven words: *From ignorance through love to the furthest shore.* That middle word — love — is where Wayism parts company with traditions that frame the journey as knowledge alone.
